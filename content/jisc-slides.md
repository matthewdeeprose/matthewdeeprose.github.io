## SSR and AER access for teaching staff

Before
A fragmented hunt
Information spread across SharePoint and network drives.
Access problems because the data was confidential.
Staff had to know where to look and how to get in.
After
Delivered inside the VLE
AER and SSR information appears where staff already teach.
Permissions applied automatically by instructor role.
Access is immediate, with no manual steps for the user.
AER: Additional Exam Recommendations, SSR: Student Support Recommendations

> Every teacher benefits — not just those teaching disabled students.
>
> Additional Exam Recommednations are accommodations that may be made available to Students with a disability, long-term health condition, mental health condition or Specific Learning Difference (SpLD)
> Examples include extra time, rest breaks and the opportunity to take an exam in smaller rooms.
>
> Student Support Recommendations (SSRs) are "Reasonable adjustments" that are recommended to support students during their time at the University, particularly students with an impairment, disability or Specific Learning Difference. They are Written by Specialist Practitioners in SD&I team following consultation with student and documentary medical evidence.

# Slide number: 28
## Were we transforming the right thing?

Accessibility
Every person in the chain handles a Word document with barriers. Completed reports reach SharePoint, and those barriers can pass through to students.
Efficiency
Between 11 and 15 email handoffs per form. Copies land in seven or more managed locations, each maintained by a different team. No automation.
Governance
Oversight committees work from incomplete data. Staff shortages mean only two of all sections get transferred into the spreadsheet.

# Slide number: 29
## Same scaffolding, two users

Screen readers and AI browser agents both navigate via the accessibility tree — the structured representation the browser builds from semantic HTML and ARIA.
Screen reader users
Need machine-readable semantics to know what each element is and does. Without proper markup, navigation becomes guesswork.
Ai agents
Need the same machine-readable semantics to act reliably. Production examples: ChatGPT Atlas, Playwright MCP
An accessible redesign serves both audiences at once.

## Barriers sit in systems. Inclusive data flows keep them from forming.

Preferred names
Specify for each data integration whether the system needs the legal name or the preferred first name.

Example: dead-naming for trans students, or name changes for safety.


Data minimisation
Send each system only the data it needs for its business function — no default inheritance of every field.
Example: does the system need to know a person’s title?

## What do these examples share?

One redesign, three wins
A single redesign could fix accessibility, efficiency, and governance at once.

Institutional, not individual
Accessible design embeds reasonable adjustments at the system level. 
The Abrahart ruling raised the need to preempt adjustments rather than wait for each individual request.
Accessibility as a lever
Starting from accessibility can reveal operational improvements nobody was measuring. 
As inclusive design reduces the need for individual adjustments, the count of disclosures stops being the right measure of success – we'll need a new one.

# Slide number: 38
## The wider barrier

Handwritten content blocks supports that many disabled groups need.
The Barrier
An image carries no structure
Handwritten, scanned, or photographed content is invisible to assistive technology. It cannot be read aloud, resized, recoloured, or passed on for further processing.
What gets unlocked
Many supports require structured digital content
Text-to-speech, screen magnification, font and colour changes, EPUB readers for Kindle, translation, search, and integration with other assistive technology — all need structure to work, and many disabled groups depend on them.
Maths — the hardest case
The structured form for maths is MathML
Reading an equation out as a flat sentence is impossible to parse. 
MathML provides hierarchical semantic notation that lets a blind student navigate element by element.

> The process begins on the left with "Upload PDF & choose engine," marking the initial preparation where a document is submitted for conversion. In the next step, "MathPix OCR," artificial intelligence reads and digitises the uploaded content, including text and mathematical expressions. Following this, the user enters the "Review confidence map" stage, which assesses the accuracy and identifies potential errors before proceeding information.
>
> From here, a user may enter an optional 'Refinement loop' for iterative improvements. This loop consists of two main refinement branches and one alternative pathway. Users might first invoke "AI enhancement," a process informed by three distinct data feeds—structural inventory, semantic context, and curated prompts—each providing targeted inputs to optimize transcription quality. Subsequently, users could apply a "Manual MMD edit" for direct correction and modification of the output. As an alternative to this iterative refinement, at any point within this loop, one may choose to "Save session (zip)" a point that branches out of the main sequence to export the current work-in-progress.
>
> Leaving the refinement loop, the process continues to "Convert to target format," where the digitised document is exported into a wide range of standard formats, including HTML, DOCX, for presentations, PDFs, or LaTeX files. Finally, the user concludes with "Final adjustments," providing opportunities for last refinements and quality checks on the formatted output.
