What is colour contrast?

Contrast measures how easy it is to tell text apart from its background. High contrast means the text stands out clearly. Low contrast makes it hard to read, especially for people with low vision, colour vision deficiency, or anyone reading a screen in bright sunlight.

We measure contrast as a ratio. A ratio of 1:1 means no contrast at all (white text on a white background). A ratio of 21:1 is the maximum (black on white).

The rules you need to follow

The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) set the standards. The University aims for AA compliance as a minimum. Here is what that means for you:

AA (4.5:1) — Normal body text must have a contrast ratio of at least 4.5:1 against its background.

AA Large (3:1) — Text at 18pt (24px) or above, or 14pt (18.66px) bold, needs at least 3:1. This threshold also applies to graphical objects and user interface components such as icons, borders, and form controls.

Watch out: The 3:1 ratio is only valid for text that meets the large text size threshold. If your text is smaller than 18pt regular or 14pt bold, you must use the full 4.5:1 AA ratio. When in doubt, aim for 4.5:1.

AAA (7:1) — The highest standard. Aim for this where possible, especially for body text.

What this means for you

When you work in PowerPoint, Word, Canva, or InDesign, you control which colours you place text on. Use the quick lookup to check your background colour, then pick a text colour from the passing options.

Tip: The safest combinations are White (#fafafa) or Black (#111111) text on your chosen background. For coloured text on coloured backgrounds, always check first.

You set colour values in CSS, so you have precise control. Use the pair builder to test specific combinations and get the exact hex values. Copy the values straight into your stylesheets.

Tip: Test interactive states too. Hover colours, focus indicators, and placeholder text all need to meet contrast requirements.

You might not choose colours yourself, but you can spot problems. If text on a slide or webpage looks hard to read, it probably fails the contrast requirements. Flag it with your communications or web team.

Tip: The simplest rule: dark text on light backgrounds, or light text on dark backgrounds. Avoid mid-tone text on mid-tone backgrounds.

Graphical objects need contrast too

The 3:1 minimum contrast ratio does not only apply to large text. It also covers graphical objects and user interface components — icons, chart elements, form field borders, focus indicators, and any visual element a user needs to perceive in order to understand or operate the interface.

Tip: If you use icons in your designs, make sure the icon colour has at least 3:1 contrast against its background. The same applies to chart segments, infographic elements, and the borders of form fields.
